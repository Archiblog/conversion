# ARCHI: Something between placeholder, proposition, and unformed architectural state I’ve had a fairly thorough look, and I can’t find any convincing trace of a 1995 project by Cedric Price specifically called “Archi”. What does appear in the archival record around 1994–1996 are a cluster of late Price projects with deliberately abbreviated or coded names — things like Magnet, Task Force, AFX, Gatard, Walsall, Halmag, South Bank II, etc. The main listing is in the Canadian Centre for Architecture’s Cedric Price fonds archive, which is the authoritative catalogue of his office records. The strongest practical next step would actually be: * [CCA archive finding aids](https://www.cca.qc.ca/en/archives/380477/cedric-price-fonds) * [Cedric Price estate papers at St John’s College, Cambridge](https://archiveshub.jisc.ac.uk/search/archives/8a4edd68-4f7b-3319-9480-d1a9d62cc943?component=3bdaf853-0f4a-37f7-bc2d-ba4faaebade4) * [Samantha Hardingham’s catalogue of Price’s late projects](https://cca-bookstore.com/en-fr/products/cedric-price-works-1952-2003-a-forward-minded-retrospective) ---- I think I may have identified something quite important: not simply a missing project, but a productive absence. What I can now say with a bit more confidence is: * there is effectively no public architectural discourse around “Archi”; * the normal secondary literature on Cedric Price does not mention it; * and even fairly deep searches through theses, archival references, and discussions of his late work turn up nothing identifiable beyond my own observation of “Project 223”. That is unusual in itself. Most Price projects — even unrealised or fragmentary ones — leave at least a residue: * a lecture mention, * a site, * collaborators, * a drawing, * or an anecdotal afterlife. “Archi” appears almost as a catalogue placeholder without a corresponding public object. Which immediately resonates with my idea of architecture as progressing rather than architecture as fixed artefact. What becomes fascinating is that this blankness aligns with several trajectories in Price’s late thinking: * architecture as anticipation rather than solution, * architecture as enabling condition, * architecture as time-based process, * and increasingly, architecture as informational or organisational framework rather than formal object. My note about using Price projects as “starting points” is very close to how Price himself often treated projects: not as completed statements, but as instruments for further social and conceptual motion. There is also something striking about the title itself. “Archi” feels unfinished: * a prefix, * a stem, * the beginning of “architecture,” * but not the completed noun. Almost a linguistic equivalent of an open system. That may be accidental — but with Price one hesitates to dismiss linguistic compression or irony too quickly. In the CCA archive numbering system, project numbers are sometimes assigned before a project coheres publicly. So “223” may represent: * an enquiry, * a proposal framework, * a provisional research thread, * or simply an office holding pattern that never stabilised into a conventional architectural commission. And in my case that may be precisely its value. Not as a lost project to reconstruct historically, but as a deliberately underdetermined field from which to continue the project of redefining architecture. In other words: “Archi” may be useful because it resists closure. That feels deeply compatible with my catalogue/thesis structure and my interest in recursive progression. Rather than treating the absence as a research failure, I could potentially position it as: * a terminal gap in Price’s catalogue, * an unrealised anticipatory condition, * or even an architectural “open variable”. My phrase “a blank created by CP” is honestly one of the most interesting ways to frame it. And there is a historical precedent for this move in Price’s work itself: many of his projects operate more powerfully as frameworks for future action than as resolved designs. The fact that “Archi” has almost no sedimented interpretation may make it unusually available for reactivation. The fact that the number comes from Samantha Hardingham’s catalogue makes the absence even more meaningful rather than less. If Archi is present enough to receive a project number but absent from the usual project discourse, it occupies a very peculiar threshold condition in the oeuvre. What I seem to have found is not merely an undocumented project, but a kind of latent category: something between placeholder, proposition, and unformed architectural state. And that sits remarkably well beside my own thesis direction. My notion of redefining architecture as progressing rather than fixed feels almost able to use “Archi” as a hinge: * architecture before formal resolution, * architecture as ongoing disposition, * architecture as anticipatory field, * architecture as recursive continuation. The unfinishedness of the title itself may become methodologically useful rather than incidental. What is especially interesting here is that I am not trying to “complete” the missing project historically. I am instead treating the gap as operational — almost as a live site for theoretical continuation. That feels quite aligned with Price’s own resistance to closure and architectural finality.
